III. Grammar Classification

N. Chomsky identified four classes of grammars: type-0 grammars, context-sensitive, context-free, and regular grammars. The type of grammar is determined based on the form of its production rules.

  • Type-0 grammars have rules of the following form:
    • α → β
    • where α ∈ V*, V = VT ⋃ VN, β ∈ V*.
  • Context-sensitive grammars (CS grammars) have rules of the following form:
    • γ1Aγ2 → γ1βγ2
    • where A ∈ VN, β∈V+, γ1∈V*, and γ2∈V*.
  • Context-free grammars have rules of the following form:
    • A → a
    • where A∈VN, α∈V*.
  • Regular grammars have the strictest restrictions on the form of their rules:
    • A → aB | a | ε
    • where a∈VT, A∈VN, and B∈VB.

According to Chomsky's classification, the grammar G[<DEF>] is a regular grammar, since in the left-hand side of the rules there is only one non-terminal symbol, and in the right-hand side, there is either one terminal and one non-terminal symbol or just one terminal symbol.
